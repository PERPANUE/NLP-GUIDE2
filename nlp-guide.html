Natural Language Processing

Teaching machines
to read language

NLP is the field that gives computers the ability to understand, interpret, and work with human language. This is the story of how we got from counting words to machines that read like humans.

The Problem

Why is language hard for machines?

Computers understand numbers. Language is messy, ambiguous, and context-dependent. The same word means different things in different sentences. Sarcasm, idioms, synonyms — none of it maps neatly to math.

Ambiguity

"I saw her duck" — did she duck, or was there a duck?

Context

"Bank" means something completely different near "river" vs "money."

Sarcasm

"Oh great, another Monday" is negative, but the words sound positive.

Scale

Billions of documents exist. No human can label them all by hand.

The History

How we got here

NLP did not arrive fully formed. It evolved over decades, each era solving the failures of the one before it.

1950s — Rule-Based Systems

Early NLP was hand-coded rules. Linguists wrote thousands of if-then statements. "If the sentence contains 'not', flip the sentiment." Brittle, expensive, and impossible to scale.

1990s — Statistical NLP

Instead of rules, let the machine learn patterns from large text corpora. Count word frequencies, build probability models. Better, but still treating words as isolated units with no meaning.

2013 — Word Embeddings

Word2Vec arrived. Words became vectors in a mathematical space. Similar words cluster together. "King minus man plus woman equals queen." Language finally had geometry.

2017 — Transformers

The paper "Attention Is All You Need" changed everything. Instead of reading left to right, models could now look at all words simultaneously and weigh their relationships. Context became computable.

2018 — BERT & Pretraining

Google trained a Transformer on the entire Wikipedia. Instead of building a model per task, you pretrain once and adapt to anything. The modern NLP era begins.

Chapter Two

Text Classification

Given a piece of text, assign it a label. That is the entire task. The challenge is teaching a machine what patterns in language correspond to which categories.

The Task

What classification actually is

You feed in text. The model outputs a category. Behind the scenes, the model converts the text to numbers, runs it through a learned function, and returns the most likely label.

Approach 1

Bag of Words

The oldest approach. Take a sentence, throw away the order, and count how many times each word appears. That count vector becomes the input to a classifier.

Example

"The cat sat on the mat"

becomes → { the:2, cat:1, sat:1, on:1, mat:1 }

The problem. "The dog bit the man" and "The man bit the dog" produce the exact same vector. Word order — which carries meaning — is completely discarded.
Approach 2

TF-IDF — Smarter Counting

TF-IDF improves on BoW by asking: is this word actually useful, or does it appear in every document? Common words like "the" and "is" are downweighted. Rare, meaningful words like "bankruptcy" or "touchdown" get upweighted.

Intuition

"The" appears in 10,000 documents — low weight. "Bankruptcy" appears in 12 documents — high weight. The rare word tells you more about what the document is actually about.

Still the same core problem. "Bank" in a finance article and "bank" near "river" get the same score. Context is still invisible.
Approach 3

Word Embeddings

Instead of counts, represent each word as a point in a mathematical space — a list of 300 numbers called a vector. Words with similar meanings cluster near each other in this space.

Word Similarity in Vector Space
king queen prince dog cat puppy happy joyful glad
same color = similar meaning = close in vector space
The Famous Equation

king − man + woman ≈ queen

The embedding space captures relationships between concepts. You can do arithmetic on meaning.

Still one problem left. These are static embeddings. Every word gets one fixed vector forever. "Bank" always sits in the same spot — regardless of whether you mean a financial institution or a riverbank.

Measuring Performance

Is your model actually good?

Accuracy alone is misleading. If 95% of your emails are not spam, a model that always says "not spam" scores 95% accuracy — while being completely useless.

MetricWhat it measuresUse when
AccuracyOverall correct predictionsClasses are balanced
PrecisionOf all positive predictions, how many were rightFalse alarms are costly
RecallOf all actual positives, how many did you catchMissing cases is dangerous
F1 ScoreBalance of precision and recallImbalanced datasets — default choice
Default rule. When in doubt, optimize for F1. It gives equal weight to false positives and false negatives, and does not reward models that simply predict the majority class.

Classification is solved. Now — how do modern models actually understand context? That is where pretrained encoders come in.

Chapter Three

Pretrained Encoders

Large neural networks trained on massive text corpora. They produce contextual representations — the same word gets a different vector depending on its surrounding context. This is the state of the art.

The Architecture

The Transformer

Introduced in 2017. The key innovation is self-attention: instead of reading words one by one, every word looks at every other word simultaneously and learns which ones to pay attention to.

Self-Attention — What does "it" refer to?
hover the words to see attention weights
Why this matters. "Bank" near "cash" and "money" gets a different representation than "bank" near "river" and "fish." Context is finally computable.
The Model

BERT — Pretrained on Everything

Google trained a Transformer on all of Wikipedia and thousands of books using two self-supervised tasks — no human labels needed. The result is a model with deep general language understanding that can be adapted to almost any task.

Masked Language Modeling

15% of words are hidden. The model learns to predict them — forcing genuine bidirectional understanding.

Next Sentence Prediction

Given two sentences, predict whether B follows A. Teaches discourse and paragraph-level understanding.

How Tokenization Works

Before BERT can process text, words must become numbers. BERT uses subword tokenization — it splits unknown words into known fragments, so it can handle any word it has never seen before.

Subword Splitting

"unaffordable" → un + ##afford + ##able

The ## prefix means "this chunk continues from the previous token."

Special Tokens

[CLS] The film was un ##for ##get ##table [SEP] [PAD] [PAD]
The attention mask. Padding tokens are added to make batches the same length. The attention mask tells BERT which tokens are real (1) and which are padding (0). Forgetting this silently degrades your results.
Adapting BERT

Fine-Tuning

BERT understands language generally. Fine-tuning adapts it to your specific task using your labeled data. Think of it as BERT's first week on the job — a short training period on your domain.

What Fine-Tuning Looks Like

"This product broke immediately" → Negative

"Absolutely love it, 5 stars" → Positive

"Delivery was late but product ok" → Neutral

Feed these to BERT for 2–5 training epochs. It learns to map your text to your categories using everything it already knows about language.

Advanced

Zero-Shot — No Labels Needed

Modern models can classify text into categories they have never been trained on. You describe the categories in plain English. The model uses its language understanding to reason about whether the text matches.

How It Works

Text: "Interest rates are rising sharply"

The model asks: "Is this text about finance?" → Yes, 94%

The model asks: "Is this text about sports?" → No, 3%

The model asks: "Is this text about technology?" → No, 3%

Why this is powerful. You can spin up a classifier for a new task in seconds — no data collection, no training, no labeling. Just describe what you want in plain English.
The Full Picture

The Journey

Every step in NLP history solved the biggest weakness of the step before it.

Evolution

Bag of Words — words are counts. Order is lost.

TF-IDF — smarter counts. Rare words matter more.

Word2Vec — words have meaning. Similar words cluster together.

Transformers — words have context. Meaning depends on surroundings.

BERT — pretrained on everything. Adapt to any task cheaply.

Zero-Shot / LoRA — efficient, flexible, no labels required.
